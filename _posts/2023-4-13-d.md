MicroRNAs (miRNAs) are small noncoding RNAs that regulate gene expression by binding to the 3′ untranslated regions of target messenger RNAs (mRNAs) and inhibiting their translation into protein (1). MiRNAs are important for the development and function of multiple cell types, including T cells (1). For example, the miRNA miR-146a is required for homeostasis of regulatory T cells (Tregs) and the suppression of inflammation (2, 3), whereas miR-155 is necessary for the differentiation of T helper 1 (Th1) and T follicular helper (Tfh) cells and the regulation of immune responses (4–6).

Type 2 helper T (Th2) cells are important for immune responses against helminth infections and allergens (7). These cells produce characteristic cytokines, such as interleukin-4 (IL-4), IL-5, and IL-13, which have critical roles in promoting mucosal immunity, eosinophilia, and smooth muscle hyperresponsiveness (8, 9). Although several transcription factors have been implicated in the differentiation and function of Th2 cells, including GATA-binding protein 3 (GATA3), interferon regulatory factor 4 (IRF4), and nuclear factor of activated T cells (10, 11), the role of miRNAs in regulating Th2 responses is poorly understood. MiR-21 has been reported to support the activity of IL-4 in mouse Th2 cells (12), but the function of other miRNAs in human Th2 cells has not been investigated in depth.

To define the role of miRNAs in human Th2 cells, we sorted CD4+ T cells from human lung tissue and differentiated them into Th2 cells with IL-4 and IL-13. These cells produced IL-4, IL-5, and IL-13 and expressed the Th2-specific transcription factors GATA3 and IRF4 (fig. S1). We analyzed miRNA expression in these cells compared with nonpolarized (Thp) CD4+ T cells by using a modified version of the TaqMan Low-Density Array (TLDA) miRNA profiling assay that we have previously described (13, 14). Of the miRNAs that were differentially expressed in Th2 cells versus Thp cells, miR-205 was the most striking, with an expression level 39 times higher than that in Thp cells (P < 0.01) (Fig. 1A and table S1). MiR-205 was also strongly induced in human Th2 cells in response to antigen stimulation, as assessed by flow cytometry, with a maximum induction at day 2 of differentiation (P < 0.0001) (Fig. 1B and fig. S2A). MiR-205 was not induced in human Th1 cells, indicating that its regulation is specific to Th2 cells (P < 0.0001) (Fig. 1C). We also found that miR-205 was induced in human Th2 cells in response to the Th2-inducing cytokine thymic stromal lymphopoietin (TSLP), although to a lesser extent than in response to antigen (fig. S2B), suggesting that both antigen-specific and cytokine-mediated signals are important for the induction of miR-205 in Th2 cells.

Scientists have discovered new information about how the human immune system is changing its response to the SARS-CoV-2 virus over time. A team of researchers examined blood samples from 50 individuals who had recovered from a mild case of COVID-19 during the first wave of the pandemic in Switzerland. They found that while the levels of neutralizing antibodies in the blood declined over time, the antibodies appeared to evolve to better target certain parts of the virus. This may be good news for vaccines as it suggests that our immune systems are learning to recognize and respond to the virus in a more effective way.

The study focused on the neutralizing antibodies in the blood, which are a type of protein that the immune system produces in response to a virus invasion. These antibodies attach to the virus and block it from infecting healthy cells. The researchers observed that the levels of neutralizing antibodies peaked around two weeks after symptoms appeared, but gradually decreased over the following months. However, they also noticed that the antibodies evolved in a way that made them better able to target the SARS-CoV-2 virus. Specifically, the antibodies appeared to target a different part of the virus, called the spike protein, more effectively than they did initially.

The researchers tested the blood samples against several variants of the virus, including the Alpha and Beta variants, which are known to be more infectious than the original strain. Despite the decline in neutralizing antibodies over time, the researchers found that the blood samples still had some ability to neutralize these new variants.

The findings suggest that the immune system is continually adapting to better protect against SARS-CoV-2, even as the virus itself is changing. This could be good news for vaccine development, as it suggests that vaccines may be effective against new variants of the virus even if neutralizing antibody levels decline over time.

However, the study has some limitations. It only examined individuals who had a mild case of COVID-19, so it is not clear if the same immune response would occur in people with more severe cases. The study also did not examine the effectiveness of vaccines, which induce a different immune response than natural infection.

In conclusion, the study suggests that the human immune system is evolving to better target the SARS-CoV-2 virus over time, even as new variants emerge. While neutralizing antibody levels may decline over time, this does not necessarily mean that the immune system is becoming less effective at fighting the virus. This information may inform future vaccine development efforts and improve our understanding of how the immune system responds to viral infections over time.
